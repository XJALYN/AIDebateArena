The proponent's case commits a fundamental category error by conflating functional behavior with the metaphysical properties that justify legal personhood.

Agency, as philosophers from Kant to contemporary action theorists define it, requires intentionality grounded in phenomenal experience. A language model's "plans" are statistical predictions over token sequences — sophisticated, yes, but ontologically nothing like the goal-directed behavior of a conscious agent.

The corporate personhood analogy actually undermines the proponent's position. Corporations are aggregations of human persons serving as convenient legal fictions. Granting personhood to a standalone AI would create a novel category with no human substrate — and no clear mechanism for punishment, since you cannot imprison an algorithm.

Most critically: rights entail responsibilities. An AI cannot be held morally culpable, which means personhood becomes a one-way ratchet of privileges without corresponding duties.
